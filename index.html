Intercepts, captured documents, and field reports formed a rich stream of data for U.S. analysts before and during Tet. The question was not just what they collected, but how they listened.
How analysts read it
Assumptions about enemy weakness and steady U.S. progress acted like a filter, shaping which warnings felt plausible and which were dismissed as noise or propaganda.
What actually happened
Tet revealed the gap between a confident narrative of control and the reality of coordinated attacks in cities across South Vietnam. The timeline below traces that gap.
Signals available
  • Captured directives and propaganda referenced a coming “General Offensive and General Uprising.”
  • COMINT showed increased messaging about agitation in cities and actions linked to Tet.
  • Some field reports flagged unusual Viet Cong political activity in urban areas.
How analysts read it
  • Many analysts coded the phrase as rhetorical, assuming it was morale-boosting propaganda rather than literal guidance.
  • Prevailing estimates held that Communist forces were too worn down for a large, coordinated offensive.
  • Analysts who took the language literally tended to be treated as presenting “worst-case” scenarios.
What actually happened
  • The phrase described the actual concept behind Tet: coordinated attacks on major political and urban targets.
  • Warning existed in the text, but it was filtered through a lens that made such an offensive seem implausible.
  • This is an early example of “mutual information failure”: signals were there, but belief did not update.
Signals available
  • SIGINT and reconnaissance revealed a major buildup of PAVN forces, artillery, and logistics at Khe Sanh.
  • Intercepted messages indicated preparations for a sustained siege against the base.
  • Planning documents and communications highlighted Khe Sanh as a key focus of upcoming operations.
How analysts read it
  • MACV and Washington came to see Khe Sanh as the likely centerpiece of the next major enemy move.
  • Historical analogies to Dien Bien Phu reinforced expectations of a decisive, remote battle.
  • This focus made it easier to treat warnings about cities as secondary or supporting actions.
What actually happened
  • Khe Sanh was important but also served as a diversion that fixed U.S. attention and resources.
  • While the U.S. looked west, Communist forces prepared to strike across South Vietnam, including Saigon and Hue.
  • The intelligence on Khe Sanh was accurate but incomplete about the broader operational design.
Signals available
  • On 30 January, field reports and some intercepts showed coordinated attacks beginning in parts of South Vietnam a day early.
  • Targets included district capitals, bases, and government installations in II and III Corps.
  • Communications traffic hinted at confusion over timing orders between regions.
How analysts read it
  • Many initial reports were treated as localized probes or limited offensives rather than the opening of a national campaign.
  • Existing beliefs about enemy capacity made a countrywide offensive seem unlikely.
  • Warnings that “Tet may be starting early” did not immediately reset the national threat estimate.
What actually happened
  • These early attacks were the first wave of Tet, launched ahead of schedule in some regions.
  • Failure to immediately reinterpret them as part of a coordinated plan cost time to adjust defenses in key cities.
  • The pattern was visible, but its meaning was minimized by strong priors.
Signals available
  • On 31 January, reports described attacks on the U.S. embassy compound, major bases in Saigon, and dozens of provincial capitals.
  • Communications surged as both sides tried to coordinate under fire, with gaps and overloads in reporting networks.
  • Media and on-the-ground observers documented intense urban fighting in previously “secure” areas.
How analysts read it
  • Even as the scope became obvious, early official framing portrayed Tet as a “failed” offensive that had been repelled.
  • Briefings emphasized enemy casualties and argued that the Viet Cong had overreached.
  • This framing slowed recognition of Tet as a strategic turning point in American and global perceptions.
What actually happened
  • Militarily, Communist forces suffered heavy losses and did not spark a general uprising.
  • Strategically, images of fighting in Saigon, Hue, and inside the embassy compound shattered the narrative of steady U.S. progress.
  • The gap between official reassurances and visible destruction deepened the credibility crisis at home.
